The French inventor Nicolas-Joseph Cugnot built the first steam-powered road vehicle in 1769, while the Swiss inventor François Isaac de Rivaz designed and constructed the first internal combustion-powered automobile in 1808.
The modern car—a practical, marketable automobile for everyday use—was invented in 1886, when the German inventor Carl Benz patented his Benz Patent-Motorwagen.
Commercial cars became widely available during the 20th century. The 1901 Oldsmobile Curved Dash and the 1908 Ford Model T, both American cars, are widely considered the first mass-produced[3][4] and mass-affordable[5][6][7] cars, respectively. Cars were rapidly adopted in the US, where they replaced horse-drawn carriages.[8] In Europe and other parts of the world, demand for automobiles did not increase until after World War II.[9] In the 21st century, car usage is still increasing rapidly, especially in China, India, and other newly industrialised countries.[10][11]

The world is facing a serious climate crisis, but there are many solutions that can help reduce greenhouse gas emissions and create a more sustainable future. Organizations like Project Drawdown have identified over 100 ways to address climate change, ranging from clean energy transitions to better urban planning. One of the most effective solutions is shifting to renewable energy sources. Solar and wind power help reduce dependence on fossil fuels, lowering pollution and greenhouse gas emissions. Geothermal power is another promising option, harnessing Earth's natural heat to generate electricity. Electric vehicles also play a crucial role, as they produce significantly fewer emissions compared to traditional petrol or diesel-powered cars. In agriculture, regenerative farming is a powerful strategy that maintains healthy soil while absorbing carbon from the atmosphere. Another key focus area is food waste reduction, which helps conserve valuable resources and reduces unnecessary emissions. Protecting ecosystems is equally important—forests act as natural carbon sinks, absorbing CO₂, so halting deforestation is essential. Coastal mangroves also serve a critical function by safeguarding land from storms while capturing carbon..
